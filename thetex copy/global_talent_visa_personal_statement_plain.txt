Personal Statement for Global Talent Visa Application

Introduction and Research Excellence

I am Deborah Akuoko, a researcher specialising in optical sensing, computer vision, and material detection using Single-Photon Avalanche Diode (SPAD) technology. I graduated as the overall best student in my class and as the overall best graduating Master's student, which earned me my PhD scholarship at the University of Edinburgh's School of Engineering. My doctoral research developed novel frameworks advancing material-aware perception systems, addressing fundamental challenges in robotics and autonomous systems by enabling machines to distinguish material composition through temporal signal analysis - a capability elusive in conventional computer vision.

My work introduced the Particle Wave Detection (PAWD) framework, formalising the dual wave-particle nature of electromagnetic energy in optical sensing. This theoretical contribution provides a unified approach to organising signal acquisition and processing strategies, with practical applications in long-range material detection, spatiotemporal object detection, and liquid purity assessment - each demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.

Key Research Contributions

My contributions span theory, implementation, and applications:

Theoretical Innovation: The PAWD framework organises sensing pipelines along intensity versus time-resolved measurement and spectral versus non-spectral processing axes, enabling explicit reasoning about information exploitation in optical sensing systems.

Technical Achievements: I developed algorithms for flat homogeneous material detection that model subsurface scattering and reflectivity, extracting material identity from single-pixel direct time-of-flight (dToF) histograms. This enables robust classification across arbitrary standoff distances, poses, and view angles.

Advanced Systems: I created a hybrid spatiotemporal object detection system fusing one-dimensional SPAD transients with two-dimensional active pixel sensor (APS) imagery. This integration of structural cues (depth, scattering, composition) with spatial appearance dramatically improves reliability under ambiguity or spoofing - critical for autonomous vehicles and robotic manipulation.

Real-World Applications: My liquid purity assessment work demonstrates how time-resolved SPAD transients capture microscopic scattering changes from adulterants, enabling non-invasive purity assessment without chemical assays.

Research Impact and Recognition

My research addresses fundamental limitations where existing methods fail to distinguish material composition from visual appearance. By leveraging temporal signal analysis as an alternative to spectral methods, I demonstrated that SPAD-based systems achieve comparable or superior material discrimination with reduced complexity and cost. This work has been recognised through working papers, custom benchmarks, practical tools, and validation across diverse material categories demonstrating robustness against adversarial examples.
